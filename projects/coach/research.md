# Coaching Methodology

Research and rationale behind each analysis section.

## Cardiac Drift

Based on Coyle & Gonzalez-Alonso research. During steady-state aerobic exercise, heart rate
gradually rises even at constant pace due to plasma volume shifts and thermoregulation.

- **Measurement**: Compare average HR in first half vs second half of a run (excluding 5min
  warmup and 2min cooldown).
- **Threshold**: >5% drift over 60min indicates aerobic base needs work.
- **Implication**: High drift suggests the runner is relying too heavily on carbohydrate
  metabolism and would benefit from more easy/Zone 2 running.

## CTL / ATL / TSB (Banister Impulse-Response Model)

- **CTL** (Chronic Training Load): Exponentially weighted average of daily training load,
  42-day time constant. Represents "fitness".
- **ATL** (Acute Training Load): Same but 7-day time constant. Represents "fatigue".
- **TSB** (Training Stress Balance): CTL - ATL. Represents "form".

Interpretation:
- TSB < -20: Overreaching / overtrained risk
- TSB -10 to +5: Productive training zone
- TSB > +5: Fresh but potentially detraining

Limitations: Model assumes linear dose-response, ignores individual variation, and treats
all training stress as equivalent regardless of type.

## Pace at Comparable HR (Aerobic Decoupling)

Based on Joe Friel's aerobic decoupling concept. The gold standard field test for aerobic
fitness improvement.

- **Method**: Bucket runs by average HR band (±5 bpm), compare average pace in recent 8
  weeks vs prior 8 weeks.
- **Progress**: >2% faster at same HR = genuine aerobic improvement.
- **Maintaining**: <2% change = fitness plateau (not necessarily bad).
- **Regression**: >2% slower at same HR = fitness loss, overtraining, or external factors.

This removes pace variability from terrain, weather, and effort level by controlling for
cardiac output.

## Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (Gabbett 2016)

Tim Gabbett's research on injury risk in athletes.

- **Ratio**: ATL / CTL (or weekly load / 4-week average load)
- **Sweet spot**: 0.8 - 1.3
- **Danger zone**: > 1.5 (spike in acute load relative to what the body is adapted to)
- **Too low**: < 0.8 (detraining, also associated with higher injury risk on return)

The key insight is that high chronic load is protective -- well-trained athletes tolerate
more acute load. Injury risk comes from spikes, not from high volume per se.

## HR Zone Model

7-zone LTHR-based model (as used by Intervals.icu):

| Zone | Name | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| Z1 | Recovery | Active recovery, very easy |
| Z2 | Aerobic | Base building, fat oxidation, conversational |
| Z3 | Tempo | Lactate threshold development |
| Z4 | SubThreshold | VO2max development |
| Z5 | SuperThreshold | Anaerobic capacity |
| Z6 | VO2max | Max aerobic power |
| Z7 | Anaerobic | Neuromuscular, sprint |

Zone boundaries are derived from LTHR (Lactate Threshold Heart Rate). The boundaries in
the data come directly from Intervals.icu's calculation.

## 80/20 Polarized Training

Research by Stephen Seiler shows elite endurance athletes spend roughly:
- **80%** of training time in Z1-Z2 (easy, conversational)
- **20%** in Z4+ (hard intervals, tempo, race pace)

Z3 ("no man's land") should be minimized -- it's too hard to recover from quickly but
not hard enough to drive top-end adaptations.

For recreational runners, the actual split is often closer to 50/50 or worse, with too
much time in Z3. Shifting toward 80/20 is one of the highest-impact training changes
most runners can make.
